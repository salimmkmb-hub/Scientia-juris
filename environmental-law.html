Reasons for Environmental Law
1. To provide a regulatory framework for those human activities which may undermine the vital assets that support normal economic and social life.
2. To provide a legal framework for the conservation and sustainable use of natural resources.
3. To protect the health and well-being of current and future generations.
4. To provide for appropriate legal theory to explain and guide the path of the law in environment management.
5. To promote public participation in environmental decision-making.
6. To ensure the precautionary principle is applied in environmental management.
7. To provide for the liability and compensation for environmental damage.
Functions of Environmental Law
i. Set offences and offences for causing harm to the environment which is not authorised, for example, water, air and soil.
ii. Assess, control or stop certain activities (such as land use, waste disposal and development) before they are carried out.
iii. Set standards for how activities should be carried out, for example, emission standards for vehicles and industries.
iv. Enable member of public to take a part in decision making process, for example, through public hearings and consultations.
v. Create institutions to manage the environment, include Environmental agencies.
vi. Provide for the liability and compensation for environmental damage.
vii.Create specialist courts and tribunals to deal with environmental matters, for example, NEMC and Environment tribunal, it established under Environment Management Act, Cap 191
Relationship between Environmental Law and Development
The connection between environment and development is crucial in today worldwide, because the development activities have a great impact on the environment, development activities such as agriculture, industry, mining, construction and transportation can cause pollution, deforestation, soil erosion, loss of biodiversity and climate change. Therefore, it is important to have environmental law to regulate the development activities and ensure that they are carried out in a sustainable manner that does not harm the environment. Environmental law can also promote the use of clean technologies and renewable energy sources that can reduce the negative impact of development on the environment. And also, Environment law guarantee sustainability for future generations as economic advance.
Development of Environmental Law, at international level.
It divided into three stages which are: -
(i) The first stage, which is from 1900 to 1972, it was the period of the early development of environmental law, during this period, there were few international agreements on environmental issues, and the focus was mainly on conservation and ownership of natural resources.
(ii) The second stage, which is from 1972 to 1992, it was the period of the emergence of environmental law as a distinct field of law, during this period, there were many international agreements on environmental issues, and the focus was mainly on pollution control and sustainable development. At this time includes creation of international agreements like the Stockholm Convention (1972) where the outcome was the establishment of the United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP) of 1983 Brundtland Report (1983) and the Basel Convention (1989)
(iii) The third stage, which is from 1992 to present, it is the period of the consolidation and expansion of environmental law, during this period, there have been many international agreements on environmental issues, and the focus has been on climate change, biodiversity, and the implementation of sustainable development goals. It includes the establishment of the Kyoto Protocol (1997), United Nations conference on Environment and Development (1992), Rio Summit (1992), and the Paris Agreement (2015)

(ii) Colonial period (from German & to British)
During the time of colonial rule, environmental laws and policies were developed by way of international agreements and colonial laws enactments. The earliest colonial power in Tanganyika (Tanzania Mainland) was Germany, ruled from 1885 to 1919, during this period, they enacted laws and policies that were not from international perspective deal with 'fencing and fine approach to environment management' they introduce the concept of licence to appropriate exploitation the natural resources. The administrative power was used to penetrate the local production system and either co-opt and remove indigenous population.
After the end of German rule, British took over the administration of Tanganyika from 1919 to 1961, during this period, they enacted laws and policies that were influenced by international agreements and colonial laws enactments. The British introduced the concept of conservation and sustainable use of natural resources, they also established protected areas and national parks, such as Serengeti National Park and Ngorongoro Conservation Area. They also enacted laws such as the Forest Ordinance of 1922 and the Game Ordinance of 1951, which were aimed at regulating the use of natural resources and protecting wildlife. However, these laws were often implemented in a way that marginalized indigenous communities and their traditional practices, leading to conflicts over land and resource use.

Tanzania has also ratified various international agreements on environmental issues, such as the Convention on Biological Diversity, the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change, and the Paris Agreement.

Common law is a body of unwritten laws based on legal precedents established by the courts. It is derived from judicial decisions rather than statutes or written legislation. Common law principles are developed through the interpretation of statutes and previous court decisions, and they can be used to fill gaps in statutory law or to provide guidance on how to apply statutory law in specific cases. In Tanzania, common law principles are often applied in environmental cases to determine liability for environmental harm and to establish standards for environmental protection.

I. SUSTAINABLE DEVELOPMENT PRINCIPLE
The main points under this principle are the idea of meeting the needs of the present generation without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs. This principle emphasizes the importance of balancing economic development, social development, and environmental protection to ensure that we can continue to thrive as a society while also preserving the natural resources and ecosystems that support us. Sustainable development is often seen as a key goal for environmental policy and decision-making, and it is a guiding principle for many international agreements and initiatives aimed at promoting environmental sustainability. It explain in the Brutland Report, Rio Declaration of 1982 Section 3 of EMA define what is meant by sustainable development, section 5 of EMA provides for the right for anyone to file a law suit to protect environment. II. PRECAUTIONARY PRINCIPLE
This principle it require preventing or rejecting a development project in the absence of scientific evidence of the environmental harm that the project will cause. It is based on the idea that it is better to be safe than sorry when it comes to protecting the environment, and that we should take action to prevent harm even if we do not have complete scientific certainty about the risks involved. The precautionary principle is often invoked in situations where there is a potential for significant environmental harm, such as in the case of new technologies, chemical substances, or development projects. It is a key principle in international environmental law and is often included in treaties and agreements aimed at protecting the environment. It refer under principle 15 of Rio Declaration of 1992, it show the responsibility not to entervine and the project the public harm where scientific evidence show clause of such harm, and also under section 5(3) and 7(3) of EMA, cap 191, R.E 2023. Also, in the case of United States v. Carroll Towing Co., 159 F.2d 169 (2d Cir. 1947), the court applied the precautionary principle in a case involving a barge that was damaged and caused an oil spill. The court held that the owner of the barge had a duty to take reasonable precautions to prevent harm to the environment, even if there was no scientific certainty about the risks involved. Vellore Citizens Welfare forum v. Indian Railways the precautionary principle got the standard of cutomary later national law. This principle also discussed under Bonn Convention of 1979, Organization for Economic Cooperation of 1972, Baltics convention of 1974 and the Cartagena Protocol on Biosafety of 2000.
III. POLLUTER PAYS PRINCIPLE
This principle it require that those who cause pollution should be responsible for paying the costs of cleaning up the pollution and compensating those who are affected by it. The polluter pays principle is based on the idea that those who cause environmental harm should be held accountable for their actions and should bear the costs of addressing the harm they have caused. This principle is often invoked in situations where there is a clear link between a specific polluter and the environmental harm that has occurred, such as in cases of industrial pollution or oil spills. It is a key principle in international environmental law and is often included in treaties and agreements aimed at protecting the environment. It refer under principle 16 of Rio Declaration of 1992, it show the responsibility to pay for the damage caused to the environment and also under section 5(3) and 7(3) of EMA, cap 191, R.E 2023. IV. SOVEREIGNTY OVER NATURAL RESOURCES PRINCIPLE
This principle it recognize the right of states to control and manage their own natural resources, including the right to exploit and use those resources for their own benefit. The sovereignty over natural resources principle is based on the idea that states have the right to control and manage their own natural resources, and that they should be able to use those resources to promote their own economic development and well-being. This principle is often invoked in situations where there is a conflict between the rights of states to control their own natural resources and the need to protect the environment and promote sustainable development. It refer under principle 2 and 21 of Rio Declaration of 1992, UNGA resolution 1803 (XVII) of 1962, and the Stockholm Declaration of 1972, Article 9(c) of Constitution of United Republic of Tanzania of 1977, it show the right of states to exploit their own resources pursuant to their own environmental and developmental policies, and also under section 5(3) and 7(3) of EMA, cap 191, R.E 2023. In the case of Gabcikovo-Nagymaros Project (Hungary/Slovakia), the International Court of Justice (ICJ) applied the sovereignty over natural resources principle in a case involving a dispute between Hungary and Slovakia over the construction of a dam on the Danube River. The ICJ held that both Hungary and Slovakia had the right to control and manage their own natural resources, but that they also had a duty to cooperate and ensure that their actions did not cause harm to the environment or to each other.     Public interest litigation is initiate actual by public group or individuals, Public interest litigation describes legal action brought to protect or enforce right enjoyed by member of public or large party of it. It describes legal tools which allow individuals groups and community to challenges overnment decisions and activities in a court of law for the enforcement of the public, it represent the departure from traditional judicial proceedings as litigation is not necessary filed by the aggrived person.
    This tool has been used for great social change in countries like India, Pakistan, Bangladesh, Tanzania, Uganda and etc on such diverse issue, environment, health and land concern.
    Public interest litigation is not define in most statutes, however judges have interpreted it to mean bringing a court action for or on behalf of the public interest. In the case of Bandhua Mukti Marcha v. Union of India, in this case judge Bhagwati, J define public interest litigation in the following way "Public interest litigation is not in the nature of adversal litigation but it's challenge and an opportunity to the government, and it's officers to make basic human right meaningful to the deprived and vulnerable section of the community and to assure them social and economic justice which is signature of the constitution"
    In the case of Cristopher Mtikila v. Attorney General (1993) the court observed that "Public interest litigation it is not type of litigation which is meant to satisfy the curisity of the people but it's litigation which is instituted with the disire that the court would be able to give effective relief to the whole or section of the society. The conditions which must be fulfill before the public litigation is entertain by the court is taht, the court should be in a position to give the effect and complete relief, if the effective relief can be granted the court can not entertain the public litigation".
