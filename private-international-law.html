Topic two: Theories of private international law

There still no a single and universally accepted theory that have adequately answered the theoretical basis of the private international law as most of them have been criticized for being insufficient to capture the whole lot of private international law. Here are some theories which explain in details about private international law: -
1. STATUTE THEORY
Statute Theory was propounded by Ulric Huber who laid down the following three maxims, from which he considered a sufficiently comprehensive system for the reconciliation of conflicting laws could be evolved. These are: -
a) The laws of a State have absolute force within, but only within the territorial limits of its sovereignty.
b) All persons who, whether permanently or temporarily are found within the territory of a Sovereign are deemed to be his subjects, and as such are bound by his laws.
c) By reason of Comity, however, every Sovereign admits that a law which has already operated in the country of its origin shall retain its force everywhere, provided that this will not prejudice the subjects of the Sovereign by whom its recognition is sought.
Generally this theory maintains that Personal law may be applied if it is not opposed to public policy or public order. Apparently the theory appears to be very simple. But in its practical application it presents great difficulties. The greatest difficulty lies in the determination as to which statute is personal, real or mixed. According to Cheshire & North’s Private International Law, the Statute Theory lacks a scientific basis, and affords no solid ground upon which a sound and logical system can be erected.
2. INTERNATIONAL THEORY
This maintains that there are rules of conflict of laws which are universal and common to various legal systems of the world. The most damaging criticism of this theory is that it starts on the assumption that there is uniformity in the laws of the countries on characterization of legal relations, while in fact it is not so. For instance, breach of marriage promise is regarded as breach of contract in some countries, while in some it is regarded as a tort. In such a situation it may be difficult to find out the natural seat of the legal relationship.
3. TERRITORIAL OR ACQUIRED RIGHT THEORY
Courts of sovereign state do not apply foreign law but merely recognize the consequences of the operation of a foreign law. This theory tries to reconcile the territoriality of a law and the need for private international law. Dr. Cheshire has vehemently criticized this theory as being, unnecessary‘, untrue‘, and unhelpful‘ . Criticism: This theory of Acquired Rights receives insignificant support at the present day, and it has been seriously criticized. The theory does not give room for us to try any case by reference to the law of the country where the rights existed or originated. The theory strictly says that a judge cannot directly recognize or sanction foreign laws, nor can he directly enforce foreign judgments, for it is his own territorial law which must exclusively govern all cases that require his decision. So why should he in this case have to refer to the law of the country where the rights existed?.
4. LOCAL LAW THEORY
The Local Law Theory was expounded by Walter Wheeler Cook. This theory is a slight variation from territorial theory. The gist of this theory is that the court recognizes and enforces a local right that is created by its own law. But as the dispute in question has a foreign element the court would necessarily apply the rule of the forum that would be applied in the case of a purely domestic dispute. But for reasons of social expedience and practical convenience it takes into account the laws of a foreign country in which the decisive facts have occurred. Cheshire has observed that this theory is a sterile truism. Sterile because it affords no basis for the development of a system of Private International Law . According to Cheshire, North & Fawcett, this local law theory affords no basis for the system of Private International Law. For to remind an English Judge about to try a case containing a foreign element, that whatever decision he gives, he must enforce only the law of the Forum is a technical quibble that explains nothing and solves nothing. It provides no guidance whatever as to the limits within which he must have regard to the foreign law. The local law theory is based more on judicial precedence in determining the outcome of a case, and even though the court applies its own rules to the total exclusion of all foreign rules, it is not bad altogether.
5. THE THEORY OF JUSTICE
The approach of English courts to private international law is pragmatic and ethical. It has sociological, ethical and legal aspects towards the end of justice. According to Dr. Graveson, the basis of Public International Law is sociologically, in the international need for fair treatment in the private transactions of individuals, ethically, in the desire of English courts to do justice; and legally, in the obligation of their oath in office. In essence the rules of Private International Law in England are made from the precedents with the ultimate view of doing justice . Graveson admits that this theory is not a perfectly valid theory in every case, for one cannot explain in terms of absolutes an empirically developed system like our own. The application of generally sound principles in this, as in other branches of law, may lead at times to hard individual cases . In the upshot, from the above presented theories it can be construed that despite the fact that there many theories emerged to explain the theoretical basis of conflict of law but no any theory has gained a universally recognition and acceptance as being a more convincing in explaining adequately the theoretical basis of conflict of laws. Most of these postulated theories are just coupled with a lot of criticisms a thing that gives one an impression that they are inadequate to explain the theoretical basis of conflict of laws.

The two lower courts ruled that the deceased by his conduct has abandoned the domicile of Scotland for the domicile of London and that it is a settled principle that the execution of the will is to be governed according to the law of the place where the deceased when he died, was domicile which is the law of England and it was also stated that a man cannot be said to have lost his domicile unless he has chosen another. The appellant decided to appeal to the House of Lords. The House of Lords ruled that the domicile of the deceased was in England and not in Scotland or France.
    This is the first case court say that the concept of domicide is based on preposition that no one is without domicile.

    Fundamental principle of domicile
    (a) There is no person who has no domicile.
    (b) A person cannot have two domiciles at a time but he can have two residences at a time.
    (c) Domicile denotes a territorial connection with a territorial system of law.
    (d) The burden of proof in case of an allegation of the change of domicile rests with a person who alleges such a change.
    Principles to determine a domicile of origin
    Court of law developed five (5) principles that used in assert matters related to domicile of origin
    i. A legitimate child take domicile of his father
    ii. Illegitimate and child born after her father death take the domicile of his mother
    iii. A foundling child and a child where his parent domicile are unknown, is domicile in the place where he was found or born as case may be
    iv. A domicile of adopted child take a domicile of his or her adoptive parent domicile of origin
    v. Domicile of origin does not change, a person may acquire another domicile but that is not domicile of origin
    Exception is applicable in the situation of adopted child where is acquire a new domicile of adoptive parent. And also, domicile of origin can be revived once replaced by domicile of choice and where a person abondon domicile of choice.

    A person acquired by residing in another country other than the domicile of origin with an intention of making it permanent home. It acquire after a person reside the domicile of origin.     Test to take into consideration in determining domicile of choice
    1. The actual presence of a person in that country.
    2. The person must have an intention to resign that country permanent.
    3. These two test can considered together, for example, a person may have an intention to resign in a country but if he is not present there it will be difficult to say that he has acquired a domicile of choice.
    Ways of proving an intention to acquire a domicile of choice for military personnel or asylum seekers (refugees)
    i. Express intention - where a person express his intention to acquire a domicile of choice by writing or orally, for example, in the case of military personnel who are stationed in a foreign country, they can express their intention to acquire a domicile of choice by writing to the relevant authority in the country where they are stationed.
    In the case of Ross v.     ii. Implied intention - in this a person does not ulter any word to show case is intention of residing in a place permanent, however his action come interpreted to mean or implied that such a person intention to establish permanent resident/home. For instance, purchase of piece of land in foreign country.
    Implied factors to consider when we deal with the demicile of choice
    a. The length of time where a person stay in the foreign country, if a person stay for a long time in a foreign country it may be an indication that such a person has an intention to acquire a domicile of choice.
    b. Frequent level of visit to the country of domicile of origin, if a person frequently visit his country of domicile of origin it may be an indication that such a person has no intention to acquire a domicile of choice.
    c. The nature of the person's job, if a person has a job that require him to stay in a foreign country for a long time it may be an indication that such a person has an intention to acquire a domicile of choice.
    d. A level of investment of country of origin and country of choice, if a person has a level of investment in the country of origin it may be an indication that such a person has no intention to acquire a domicile of choice.
    e. The person's family ties, if a person has a family in the country of origin it may be an indication that such a person has no intention to acquire a domicile of choice.
    f. Whether a person maintain any tyees, for instance, citizenship status of the domicile of origin, if a person maintain his citizenship status in the country of origin it may be an indication that such a person has no intention to acquire a domicile of choice.
    g. Nature or type of properties he own in the country of which alledge to have a domicile of choice, if a person own a property in the country of which he alledge to have a domicile of choice it may be an indication that such a person has an intention to acquire a domicile of choice.

    In this topic we based on two (2) aspects which are -

    2. Aspect of Enforcement
    Before the digestion of this topic there are basic terminologies to define like: -
    a. Foreign court,
    b. Foreign judgment, is judment given by aforeign judge or magistrate regarding the ground for decree.
    c. Recognition of foreign judgment, It occur when host state acknowledges a foreign judgment has been conclusive regarding thematter adjudicating the same parties without re-evaluating the evidences and submission of the part to accertain it's correctness. Recognition is often used as the sheld to prevent the same cause of action from being re-litigated. In case there is final and conclusive judgment issued by the court the part are barry from refile the case in another jurisdictional court.
    d. Enforcement, is the process of using the power of the court to realize the obligation creating under foreign judgment. In Tanzania an enforcement may refer to power of Tanzania court to endure that the judgment debtor complies with order issued in the foreign judgment normall through the execution procedure. Enforcement it is pro-active steps where by the part ask the court to compell a judgment debtor certify the obligation.
    N.B
    Not all recognized judgment can be enforced in Tanzania especially those which did not grant monentary relief.
    Theories of recognition and enforcement of judgment
    I. Committ theory, Is the based on mutual respects between two sovereign states where court recognize the foreign acts to ensure a smoth operation of international cooperations. A committ theory it emphasize on reciprocity in the sense that a recognition and enforcement of the foreign acts requires reciprocal treatments.
    II. Doctrine of Obligation, is the common law principle which view judgment from the court of compitent jurisdiction imposes a legal duty or obligation on the defendant to pay a judgment sum. This doctrine is founded on moral and legal debt which is defendant is bound to pay. For example, Tanzania court act out of court acknowledge that the new legal obligation is created and a plaintiff is obligated to recover.
    III. Acquired right theory Right which has been acquired inforeign country has be recognized and enforced in any court regardless of it's territorial, application.
    Modes of enforcement foreign judgment in Tanzania
    (i) Enforcement by way of registration
    (ii) Enforcement by a way of suit
    (iii) Enforcement through extension of judgment
    ENFORCEMENT BY WAY OF REGISTRATION
    This mode is governed by the reciprocal enforcement of foreign judgment Act, Cap 8 and it's subsidiary legislation. Enforcement under registration requires reciprocity between nation or the judgment can only be registered in Tanzania only if it comes from the country where the judgment from Tanzania are also enforced, includes Kenya, Uganda, Malawi or Zanzibar, those states were mention under Judgment extension Act.
    Conditions for the enforcement by way of registration
    a) The judgment must be final and conclusive, should be determine by the merit of the case, section 14 of Civil Procedure Code, provides the distintion of determine conclusive judgment.
    b) A judgment must be of fixed sum of money or a judgment can only be registered in Tanzania if it grants monentary awards. A judgment which provide a sum payable in taxes and other charges of like nature. For example, duties, levy or in respect of a fine and other pernalties does not qualified for a registration. It explain under section 3 of reciprocal enforcement of foreign judgment Act, Cap 8.
    c) It must come from the countries recognized to have reciprocal relations with Tanzania and from the superior court.
    d) A judgment debtor must have assets in Tanzania.
    Procedures for registration, it governed by reciprocal enforcement of foreign judgment Act and it's rules
    1. Application
    Application must be done to the High Court by way of chamber summons supported by affidavity and it's expert application.
    The respondent must only be summons to show the cause of what/why the judgment did not registered.
    The applicant must ensure he/she cover the security for cost, judgment creditors must deposit cost of their application.
    2. Determination of application by the court
    where the court will summons the judgment debtor as to why judgment should not be registered, in case the respondent show a cause a court will determine and issue rulling on it. In case there is nothing a court will proceed registering the judgment, the time kimitation is 6 years from the date of original judgment or last judgment in the appeal proceedings.
    3. Possible defences
    The defences will be rises by the respondent as provided under section 6 of reciprocal enforcement of foreign judgment Act.
    ENFORCEMENT BY WAY OF SUIT
    This enforcement is basical an enforcement under the doctrine of debt obligation, meaning that it apply only to the judgment/decree thet are not falling in the registration or extension. A judgment creditor may file a suit, in the Tanzania court with jurisdiction on the matter, in file a fresh suit the judgment debtor he/she become the plaintiff and judgmnet creditor become the defendant, the plaintiff must establish the cause of action includes debt action. Under common law this enforced by summary procedure, it evolke under order XXXV of Civil Procedure Code however, in that order did not mention suit of that nature. In the case of Willow Investment v. Mbomba Ntumba where in this case it involves a foreign judgment enforcement issued in Tanzania, the High Court of Tanzania ruled that the provision of order 5(30) of Civil Procedure Code were applicable, despite the foreign ownership of one party. The Court set the proceedings as nullities, allowing the applicant to be dismissed. The case highlight the importance of proper nexus between litigation and Tanzania for the enforcement of foreign judgments.
    ENFORCEMENT BY A WAY OF EXTENSION OF DECREE/JUDGMENT
    This mode was governed by judgment extension Act, Cap 7, it apply from the decrees obtains from High Court and subordinate Court of Kenya, Uganda, Malawi and Zanzibar.
    Condition for extension
    i. Judgment must only come from only four (4) countries, Kenya, Uganda, Malawi and Zanzibar.
    ii. The nature of decree must only be in debt, damage or cost.
    Procedures for extension
    It condected by way of transfer of decree from the foreign country to the High Court of Tanzania or the subordinate court as the case may be.

    Extension of decree is based on the similar phylosophical foundation applicable to registration of decree/judgment (Committ/Recioricity).
